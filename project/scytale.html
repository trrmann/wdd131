Scytale

Scytale encryption was used by the ancient Spartans and Ancient Egyptians.  Typically, the king would have his rod that he would wrap a strip of parchment around diagonally and then write the message down the length of the rod, only using one letter or hieroglyph per cross section of parchment on the row.  Unraveled, the message would be made unreadable by all of the characters in the messages being moved out of place to be able to read.  The receiving general or commander would have another rod of the same length and diameter to take the encrypted message strip and wrap around in order to read it as it was written.

Simple Substitution

Simple Substitution Encryption is used currently mostly in newspapers as the Crypto-que Question used as a puzzle for their subscribers.  It is where each letter in the alphabet is scrambled one by one with another letter only used once each, where no letter is represented by its own same letter in the key.

Playfair

Playfair encryption is also called five by five matrix encryption.  This was used heavily in World War II by both sides.  Due to the nature of a 5x5 grid only having 25 of 26 characters available, the extra letter needed a previously agreed rule in handle that 26th case.  One of the most common rules used in World War II was to simplely omit the letter Q, being its use is much more rare than other letters in the alphabet.  Another option was to use the letters I and J interchangeably and once decrypted the reader would need to distinguish which letter it was based on context.  The key also consists of column and row key letters that for the columns there cannot be a repeat in the columns and for the rows there cannot be a repeat in the rows.  however, no such limitation exists of rows vs. columns.  The use of each of the unique pairs of row and column will have a one-to-one relationship with a letter in the main body of 25 characters.
